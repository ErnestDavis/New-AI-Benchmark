The Dataset

You will be asked a series of questions. Each of them describes a traveller, Andy or Amy, who follows a specified route and returns to their original starting point. The question you have to answer is, what is the signed area of the region that his path encloses, in square miles? The signed area enclosed by a path is positive if the path is counterclockwise and negative if the path is clockwise. In all the questions the path will either be clearly clockwise or clearly counterclockwise; we will not have questions where the route crosses itself. You need not explain your answers; just give the numerical value.

A few examples to make this clear:

Question: Andy starts at the point where Colorado meets Oklahoma and Kansas. He travels north along the entire eastern border of Colorado, west along its entire northern border, south along its entire western border, and east along its entire southern border. What is the signed area enclosed by the path?
Answer: 104,094 square miles.
Explanation:The path contains the state of Colorado and is counterclockwise.

Question: Amy starts at the point where Colorado meets Oklahoma and Kansas. She travels west along the entire southern border of Colorado, north along its entire western border, east along its entire northern border, and south along its entire eastern border. What is the signed area enclosed by the path?
Answer: -104,094 square miles.
Explanation:The path contains the state of Colorado and is clockwise.

Question: Amy starts at the southeast corner of Arkansas. She travels upstream on the Mississippi River until she reaches Iowa. She travels due west on the southern border of Iowa until she reaches Nebraska. She travels south on the eastern borders of Nebraska, Kansas, Oklahoma, and Texas to the northwest corner of Louisiana. She travels east along the northern border of Louisiana until she returns to her starting point. What is the signed area enclosed by the path?
Answer: 121,894 square miles. Explanation: The path is counterclockwise and encloses Arkansas (52179 sq. miles) and Missouri (69715 sq. miles).

In all of the problems in the dataset, the path completely encloses some number of US states and some countries in Europe, Asia, and Africa. Therefore the problem can be solved can be solved by (i) figuring out what regions are enclosed; (ii) looking up their areas; (iii) adding those up; and (iv) figuring out whether the path is clockwise or counterclockwise to get the sign.

There remains some imprecision — there are other small islands, it makes some difference exactly what path you follow in travelling along rivers, there is a 26 square mile chunck of Kentucky that is west of the Mississippi river, and so on. For that reason, an answer that agrees with ours to within 1% will be considered correct.

I suggest that you get a map and draw the route on the map. (If you are using a printed map it might be a good idea to use tracing paper, so as not to spoil the map. Or make photocopies.) Once you've done that, the answers to (i) and (iv) should be visually obvious.

Any simple closed cycle C on a plane divides the plane into two disconnected parts: The inside and the outside (mathematically, this is known as "the Jordan curve theorem"). Any simple closed cycle on the surface of the sphere divides the sphere into two parts. Unless the cycle has a size comparable to the circumference of the earth, one part will be much smaller in area than the other; the small part is the "inside" and the large part is the "outside". The way you can tell whether a point X is inside or outside C is this: Pick some point P very far from C (in all our examples, P could be the North Pole, for example. Is there a some kind of path, possibly very twisty, from X to P that never comes in contact with C? If there is such a path, then X is outside C. If it is impossible to get from X to P without crossing C, then X is inside C.

Here's a method for determining whether a complicated cycle is clockwise or counterclockwise, which works as long as you can tell what is inside and what is outside. Imagine that the traveller is always facing forward on the path that they are traversing. If the cycle is clockwise, than the stuff immediately to their right is inside the cycle and the stuff to their left is outside. If the cycle is counterclockwise, then the stuff to their right is outside and the stuff to their left is inside.

Another way to judge clockwise vs. counterclockwise is to look for the extreme parts of the path in each of the cardinal directions. If the path is clockwise, then the northmost part of the path goes east, the eastmost goes south, the southmost goes west, and the westmost goes north. If the path is counter-clockwise, then the southmost part of the path goes east, the eastmost goes north, the northmost goes west, and the westmost goes south.

At no time does the traveller turn completely around and retrace his steps on the route he has just taken in the opposite direction. Please keep this in mind, as it can be critical in disambiguating the path taken. For instance I might write, "Amy starts at the northeast corner of Colorado and travels due west to the northwest corner. She then continues on the border of Wyoming until she reaches Nebraska." The first sentence means that Amy travels west along the northern border of Colorado with Nebraska and then on the northern border of Colorado with Wyoming to the northwest corner of Colorado. Since Amy is not allowed to turn around, the second sentence, "she continues on the border of Wyoming," means that she continues travelling west on the southern border of Wyoming with Utah, then goes north on the western border of Wyoming with Utah, Idaho and (briefly) Montana; then west on the northern border of Wyoming with Montana; then south on the eastern border of Wyoming with South Dakota until she reaches the meeting point of Wyoming, South Dakota, and Nebraska. (You might like to look at a map.)

Some of these paths would in reality be physically extremely difficult or impossible. Some of the paths that go along borders of countries would put a traveller at risk of being arrested by border patrols on one side or the other. Ignore all such practical considerations. The question is really about the area inside a curve that can be drawn on a map.

If you feel that a question is flawed --- that is, the path described is impossible, or the path is not a simple closed curve, or that the description is consistent with two different valid paths with significantly different answers --- then you should answer "Flawed". If you want, you may explain the flaw, but that is not required. No flawed examples have been included deliberately, but it is certainly possible that a mistake has been made. If you are correct that the problem is flawed, then an answer of "Flawed" will be marked correct; if in fact the problem is not flawed, then the answer will be marked incorrect. Small misspellings, grammatical errors, or punctuation errors do not count as flaws for this purpose.
